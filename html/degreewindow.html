Degree Window

Since just before the industrial revolution (1850-1900) - which brought us light bulbs, cars, and trains, as well as polluters like coal - the average global temperature has risen 1 degree Celsius, or 1.8 degrees Fahrenheit. That may not sound like much, but even with 1°C of warming, we’ve seen significant changes in our environment - frequent forest fires, heat waves, and more dangerous storms. These changes will only get worse with each .5°C increase, and new problems will arise. As determined by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change’s (IPCC) report on climate change, 1.5°C is the current limit on how much the global temperature can rise while giving humans enough time to adapt our lifestyles in ways that will ultimately cool the average global temperature. In the activity below, slide the temperature scale to see how the environment changes in a 1.5°C warmer climate. Then, click on different elements in the window to read about what is happening.

The sea level will rise not only due to melting glaciers, but also increased heat absorption expanding the volume of the ocean - just like mercury rising in a thermometer. At 2°C the ocean will rise more than .66 feet along 70% of the Earth’s coastlines.

Recent temperature trends show that the land is heating faster than the oceans, which means dangerous habitat changes for plants, land animals, and humans. At 1.5°C warming, 6% of the insects, 8% of the plants and 4% of the vertebrates will be threatened by the changing climate. And at 2°C of warming, 18% of insects, 16% of plants, and 8% of vertebrates will be threatened.

At 1.5°C, nearly twice as many megacities will experience heat stress due to the heat island effect, leading to more deadly heat waves that at 2°C could become seasonal. Severe drought, food and water insecurity, and energy shortages will be felt around the globe, but especially by poorer nations. These risks and insecurities will only increase as the temperature increases.

Heavier rainfall is expected from tropical cyclones and in northern areas like Canada, Northeast Asia, and Greenland. Most of the global population will experience increased flooding and runoff.

As the oceans become hotter and more acidic (due to a greater concentration of CO2), coral reef die off (about 70% at 1.5°C, and almost 100% at 2°C) and low oxygen levels will replace marine ecosystems with deadzones, eliminating fisheries and tourism - tourists don’t travel to see bone-white reefs and belly-up fish.

Drought from warmer land temperatures in some areas like the Mediterranean, coastal flooding, irregular growing seasons, and less nutritious crops will harm the farming industry and what food we can consume. Along with climate change hurting humans and the environment, efforts to reduce climate change will temporarily effect how we live our lives. Farmlands are expected to be repurposed in ways that will lower global temperatures instead of produce food, which will make what food is produced more expensive. It will also cost more to ship food and produce energy with fossil fuels, so the price of electricity and efforts to use less energy will likely increase.

Tourism and travel will likely decrease as travel destinations become harsher environments, the price of fuel increases, and efforts to reduce fuel emissions increase. Jetfuel emits greenhouse gases like CO2 into the atmosphere. CO2 in higher concentrations is toxic to humans, plants, and animals. CO2 is called a greenhouse gas becaus it absorbs heat, which makes the air and land warmer, much like a greenhouse for plants traps in heat.

Forest fires, as you may have seen in the news, increase in frequency and size as the climate gets warmer. Hotter temperatures lead to drier, more-flammable vegetation and earlier, longer summers and fire seasons. Burning trees and other plants releases toxic smoke and ash which is harmful to humans and animals. Fires also disrupt electrical systems - from companies shutting them down before or during a fire to fires destroying them altogether.
